\begin{abstract}
Ground-effect vehicles (GEVs) operate in a regime where aerodynamic performance and stability are strongly influenced by proximity to the ground. Despite their potential efficiency advantages, GEVs present significant challenges in airfoil selection and trim behavior, particularly when transitioning between ground-effect and free-flight conditions.\\

This study investigates the aerodynamic characteristics of selected airfoil profiles in ground effect, with the objective of identifying configurations suitable for mechanically stable GEV operation. Building upon preliminary airfoil investigations conducted during a prior academic project, three representative airfoils are selected and evaluated using a combination of reduced-order aerodynamic analysis and computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations. Parametric variations in angle of attack, Reynolds number, and height-to-chord ratio are considered to assess lift, drag, and pitching moment behavior.\\

The resulting aerodynamic data are intended to support subsequent vehicle-level modeling and stability analysis, providing a physically grounded basis for evaluating trim conditions and control feasibility in ground-effect-dominated flight regimes.
\end{abstract}
